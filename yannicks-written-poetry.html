Granola Park

8/28/2019

0 Comments

 
You never know what you got ‘till it’s gone.


The fireflies remind me in the obscurity of the pine trees: a fine green that I’ve seen under plenty a ray, but in the night seams have seemed to been tucked away. Their shapes act as a barrier to the outside. 


You can’t look back in life, only forward.


A five year old me plays in the forest clearing, and I watch from the park bench along the winding path. He chases the lightning bugs with the eclectic electric energy of a summer storm, passing quickly through the blades of grass, cutting the air with his minuscule hands and sharp wit. 


You can only hold on for so long, and then you must let go.


The child catches one between his tiny palms and keeps them shaped like a coconut. He spreads his fingers slow and it glows softer than his curiosity, as if to shed some light on the grass, the trees and all the world’s mysteries. Here for an instant and vanishing in the next, the stars of the forest blink like moments under the moon, but this one was here to stay.


You have fire in your eyes, but you must be able to see through the smoke.


Under the gazebo the habitual teenagers shed some light on trees of their own, shedding light into the corner of my eye, each breath they take sheds some to their mind.

I don’t know where I obtained such power, one that allows me to make time stop: the ability to shatter the window of continuum into a series of moments, and stretch one like putty until it feels stringy suspended in my hands. 
The rest of the world spins on, but this bubble here in this park remains as such as long as I am content as the 5 year old whose grandma watches him play in the woods she has rescued.


You can’t be everywhere at once.


I know I was not the first one to stop time, because whoever did before me stretched a second so long it became three dimensional. They, and we, began to conceptualize time as a space, and not a measurement. The places where we happened to be at a specific point, captured in our memories. Don’t look at your clock! This is the time we will remember. 

My grandma looks over at the park bench where I sit, untethered by the hands of the minute or the hour. Her grandson dials her from distant shadows, telling time there’s no avail.


You can never ask too many questions.


We talk, you and I. You’re here with me on the bench too, with eyes that have the twinkle of all the stars I can’t see.

A moment more organic than 2 co-op’s, a farmers market, and white fridges. I rap you granola bars so chewey they stay in the  mouth for days: words sweet as honey caught in the ridges, oaty oaths caught in the bridges. Gums stay sticky, as if to say stay with me, as our lips continue to form natural conversation:

the type that turns houses to homes like doctors turned detectives, in which case I’m lost in the moment and never sick of it.



You can bring a horse to the water, but you can’t make it drink.

You can do anything you put your mind to.

Your worth is not determined by your success.



Blanket statements to keep me warm

Jack Frost, or maybe Robert, will stroll through here soon enough.


For a long time I didn’t know how I feel about you. Don’t worry, in case you’re tense, I say you because you’re the second person present.
She’s still here with me too




This clearing in the darkness.

A wind that makes breeze an injustice of a word whose ease in nature understates the power that accompanies the animated Anemoi like a fleet of fleeting discussions with substance far from shore

An air that makes oxygen so much an element discovered, measured,
disseminated that it understates the power that comes with the inhalation of cosmic creation

A lighthouse that exists only in the late eyes of its beholder, understates the power of a remote mysterious beauty with seemingly little use and even less purpose, it floats on the surface of an insignificant mountain, a crevice in a seamless sea



I sit here for hours on end, wondering in its direction.



Feelings still raw like a scraped knee cap's skin from a bad trip because the angel on my shoulder has had a habit of tabbing acid and now he's bat shit . . . watching these melting suns set

Feelings still gone like wisdom teeth still on and anesthetic was an aesthetic, fat cheeks and numb tugging like the doc was the devil on my other shoulder and he wasn't done drugging, doing ecstasy next to me hugging himself: I'm seeing stars now.



Fuck, I fucked up the imagery.

I take another picture, mind more steady this time, to make for a new phone background.



A water that makes wet a trickle of fickle feeling that understates the power of the strongest bodies whose glistening
skin shines like Poseidon's eye under the moon

It wasn't until the chariot rose again that I would learn I was staring at my own all along.

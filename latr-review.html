LAtr Album Review

4/22/2018

0 Comments

 
    “There are plenty of fish in the sea.” The cliché consolation for the loss of love or the failure to find it has been said about as often as waves crash on shores. It seems every person goes through these ups and downs as parts of the ebbs and flows of life. So Maryland based rapper/songwriter Thomas Stanton (aka Txmorrow) isn’t treading any new water when he explores this concept and more in his debut mixtape LATR, an acronym for “Lotus and the Rain”. But the 21 year old is able to shrink this seemingly endless sea into an isolated pond of watery vignettes: in which he floats like the aquatic plant he references in the title. Meditating on relationships (broken and breaking), depression, and even suicide, he treats each track like a ripple from a rock that was thrown into his heart.
    The album begins with Txmorrow washing into focus, singing a warning to a “stuck up" girl about how he is probably no good for her. His vocals have an edgy warble to them that come with the emotions of ending a relationship, a rawness that make up for his vocal limitations. Riding more downcast production into the next two tracks with some vocal effects and slippery flows, Stanton is able to establish the biggest strength of his tape as a whole: the aesthetic. “The Rain” and “Dearly Beloved” are two standout tracks that outline the suicide of his friends that he had drifted apart from, and a lost love. This first third of the album create a sense of overcast growing over the pond, and as the storm brews Thomas sits with nothing more than the umbrella he holds on the album’s (creative) cover and his own reflection in the pond to protect him from what’s to come. These emotions in his lyrics, the cover art and the pain in his voice create cohesion, like the drops of rain on the pond that would later fall.
    The storm comes quickly after these two songs, as the album suddenly booms with the thunder of “Rockstar Canon”, a hard hitting banger with an aggressive beat and even more aggressive bars. Lines like “Thom cruise the way I’m whippin through traffic ….. / Tom Cruise the way you fuck niggas actin” strike quick and hot like lightning, bringing some energy to his sorrow. This two part track strays a little bit from the tape's themes lyrically, but not emotionally, as it show’s Txmorrow’s versatility as an artist. The storm almost evolves into a hurricane on the following track “H.U.R.T”, which has the most abrasive sonic palette of the tape. The beat is a standard trap affair with a distorted background vocal to give it more character. Txmorrow raps in a low tone that is both hard hitting and hard hurting, however the background vocals somewhat swallow him and the song almost drowns in itself. The switch in flows makes the song more listenable, but overall the background vocals overpower him as any hurricane would.
    For the majority of the remaining tracks on the album Thomas calms down, as if the storm passes and there are only clouds left in the sky. The skit and the interlude, “Aint Shit” and “Depression” respectively, serve as the only other voices on the album, and both are female. However they play out more as voices in Txmorrow’s head than additional characters in his narrative, supplementing his meditations rather than interrupting them. The latter half of the album sees Stanton falling into the currents of current sounds and styles, as he even acknowledges some of his influences in the track titles. The airy “XO Island” is cut beautifully with a crisp chorus that is reminiscent of Take Care era Drake and The Weeknd (hence the XO reference). This serves as Stanton’s best vocal performance and perhaps the most complete song on the tape, another standout. The song “Truthfully" also takes a page from the OVO playbook, as Txmorrow does his best PARTYNEXTDOOR impression, albeit very ably. “Heartbreak Hotel” references a lyric on Kanye West’s 808 and Heartbreaks, and sonically emanates the early partnership between West and Cudi that would go on to change hip hop. Txmorrow doesn’t break any of these molds but rather melts them into the depths of his unique pool of emotions, making them worthwhile despite sounding familiar on the surface.
    The tape is a very concise and cohesive effort, but sometimes to it’s detriment. The song “Forgiveness” has a beautiful transition from the interlude that precedes it. Its slow, grandiose sense of crescendo is supplied by a powerful piano riff, only to be cut off right when the beat and Txmorrow alike are on the edge of combusting with energy. “Treason," probably the tape’s catchiest cut, passes as quickly as the sword swipes it samples, despite its sharp beat and verse. These small drops in Txmorrow’s pond only leave us thirsty for more.
    However, this also plays to some strengths. The album’s 42 minute run time feels more like a dreamy sail than a difficult journey thanks to Txmorrow’s incredible knack for flows and melodies. Although he has no features, he is versatile enough to carry the album without it ever drying out. This is also helped along by the stellar sequencing of the tracks- something that is often difficult for young artists and veterans alike. So by the time the clouds finally clear up, the sun comes out, and Txmorrow sits there in a moment of clarity with his umbrella blown inside out on the last track, his journey is all the more rewarding. It is easy to see how he has grown even as the album has progressed. In earlier tracks he found strength in his pain, exclaiming, “I am only as strong as the passion with which y’all hate me”. But on the title track (the closing track and best track), he finds strength in his most powerful tool: his own words. “Letters Are Too Real … I think this is the first time I meant this sincerely” he says just as the album pulls to a close, like a wave retreating back into its body, subtly revealing the album title’s true meaning.
    Thomas Stanton doesn’t necessarily bring anything new to the game in terms of his rapping or music, but that was never his aim. He’s just another kid absorbing and processing the hardships of the past couple of years, looking so deep into the rain that he sees his bright future. Maybe that’s why he chose the name Txmorrow. His introduction into the music world is by no means a perfect one, but neither is love, life or the loss of either. On LATR Txmorrow captures all of this in a way that is solely his own, and shows us in his powerful pond that we are only getting our feet wet.

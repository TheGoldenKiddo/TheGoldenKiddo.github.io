Crybaby Ep Review

1/12/2019

0 Comments

 
“Crybaby” is often something we call those who complain excessively or become emotional over things of little significance. However, the term is somewhat redundant and baseless as all babies cry (as well as all adults). Human association of crying with babies most likely stems from the fact that babies have very few ways of communicating their emotions, and the act of crying is charged with the responsibility of releasing a plethora of feelings that are all as varied and complex as the spectrum of sentiments experienced by adults. When a baby cries it can be difficult for a grown up to decipher the reasons behind the baby’s tears. The baby itself may not be able to fully process its surroundings, nor its emotions, but it is expresses them through crying nonetheless, regardless of who understands them. DMV artist Txmorrow (Thomas Stanton) exemplifies this on the cover of his new EP titled after the term itself. Just eight months after his debut tape, #LATR, the recently released project depicts the artist now floating in space as opposed to the water on which he hovered on his previous project. The cover invokes an even greater sense of weightlessness and isolation present in his music than ever.


At just 10 tracks and a 33 minute run time, CRYBABY EP is much less to unpack than #LATR. Upon first impression, it is slicker sonically than its predecessor as the production value is very clean and cohesive: each song sliding into the next seamlessly. The melodies are sweet as refined sugar, more easily consumed than the rawness of #LATR. This is largely due to Txmorrow’s growth as a song writer as well as his increasing skill in sound engineering. He has matched his knack for hooks and bridges with precision behind the boards in order to best accentuate them with moving layers of vocal effects. This is immediately evident in the opening track, “SOBs”, a quick interlude with pitched up vocals a la Frank Ocean that mimic the voice of a toddler. Thematically, the song has Txmorrow “hanging ‘round some SOBs” engaging in meaningless hobbies like drinking from red cups and singing “rooftop melodies” as if to avoid the heavy things that weigh on his heart: a pleasant intro to establish the premise of the tape.


The weight soon presents itself as the tape moves into the title track “Crybaby”, a moody banger produced by Txmorrow’s high school friend and fellow DMV artist NXMVD. A simmering guitar sample set over bouncy drums allows Stanton to croon freely within the inertia of the instrumental. He calls an unknown girl a “crybaby”, accusing her of fake love that causes their relationship to fall apart. He uses impeccable adolescent imagery to pit her immaturity at the heart of his pain, belting: “Rocking vans and they taped up, I taste blood” as if the heartbreak he is facing were the neighborhood bully he ran into at the playground.


However, where #LATR seemed to be directed at one ex lover in particular, CRYBABY seems to address love more generally. It appears that Thomas has experienced a bevy of interactions with women in between the two projects and it shows. More a collection of short chapters about missed opportunities, failed relationships or flames short lived, it does not tell one cohesive story. This frees Txmorrow to express his emotions unencumbered as he feels them, similarly to how a baby does before it learns to speak. He sounds fed up with it all on “Burn Out” exclaiming “it’s hard to stop thinking ‘ bout that sad shit when you still get sad a lot” as if trying to justify his despondent ruminations to the reflection in his astronaut helmet. This self-reflection is more a result of solitude than growth, as he says “I don’t feel the love and yet they show it on the daily.” Txmorrow is in a space of his own, incapable of processing his surroundings likely because there is nothing to ground him. None of these relationships seem to work out.


As he runs through all of these memories in his head, it seems they all begin to blend together to the point where it’s hard to discern one from another. So when Stanton pleads to someone or rejects their actions it’s more like he’s talking into a void, or maybe the stars he seems to be amongst. Like a pilot who’s lost communications with those below him, on “Love Sick” he wails “I don’t wanna crash but I can’t control my falling” over downbeat production with the strum of a gentle guitar. He chants “Fuck Love” on an interlude that is the tape’s hardest hitting banger despite it lasting just over a minute. He sings “I dont know who to trust” and it’s not entirely convincing that he himself isn’t included in the scope of that statement. This was the rawest track on the tape and makes it a quick standout.


The following track, “Romeo” is easily the best on the tape, with the most stripped back instrumental Stanton’s vocals seep into themselves with despair. He sounds despondent, his low point emotionally is the high point musically as the pain comes through his voice. Lyrics about lack of connection with a girl in heart, mind, and eye causes him to turn to substances to deal with the pain. The drug induced confinement makes for most relatable passage in the pages he turns. The imagine of an astronaut getting high while already in orbit is a curious one, but Txmorrow raps from a perspective as if he is just coming down from it and is just pouring up another cup.


The tape finishes of strongly with “Ivory”, “Growing Pains” and “Alabaster”. The former is a pop-R&B-crossover that borrows heavily from an OVO/PARTYNEXTDOOR sound and aesthetic, with some galactic keys and murky bass. Stanton wishes for someone who is going to ride for him and give him a break from his anxieties. The song “Growing Pains” is a more upbeat instrumental and has the only guest feature on the project. Txmorrow does his best rapping on this track that serves as an explanation to his mother of how much he’s done since leaving her house. The chorus however is somewhat lackluster in performance. The closing track is also the longest track on the project- a 4 part epic with an instrumental that twists and turns, and Txmorrow follows suit effortlessly with his approaches to each part. He hits a divinely sinister flow on the first switch up that has a banging kick drum and soulful sample flip. As the beat continues to break down he spits a spoken word monologue about running for the police with his friends after dark. His verse fades into the final segment of the saga as a Malcolm X speech comes into focus. As the powerful figure questions the audience about the hate they have for themselves, the album draws to a close with him exclaiming that “the most disrespected person in America is the Black Woman.”


During this powerful speech the album reveals its true colors: these women that Stanton has engaged with all have a common denominator. They aren’t the crybabies, he is. Txmorrow is the one in space expressing his emotions regardless of who can hear him or understand him. Tear drops fall like the asteroid on his cover as he feels the pain that comes along with learning about himself, about others, and the relationships that link him to them. What’s most important about it all is that he’s self aware, and by the time altered vocals of “SOBs” begin to play again on the next listen, Txmorrow has convinced us that calling someone a “Crybaby” is nothing more than calling them human.
